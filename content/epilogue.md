# Epilogue: The Commons Held

You are no longer the official sitting in that first budget briefing, nodding along at unfamiliar and overwhelming information, hoping you were asking the right questions.

You know what the annual clock is telling you — and what it isn't. You know that a legally balanced [GLOSSARY:budget]budget[/GLOSSARY] and a sustainable one are not always the same thing. You know the questions that cut through complexity and get to what matters.

You know the trajectory clock is running underneath the annual numbers — accumulating obligations, eroding [GLOSSARY:assets]assets[/GLOSSARY], drifting in ways that only become visible across years. You know how to read that drift before it becomes a crisis.

And you know the generational clock — the one that extends beyond your term, your career, and even your lifetime. The one that asks not where you are or where you're headed, but what you are choosing to hand forward as a real steward of your community.

That is stewardship. Not expertise. Not perfect answers. The habit of asking the right questions, on all three clocks, for as long as you serve.

Your community's financial capacity — and therefore its well-being — is a shared resource. It was sustained or depleted by those who came before you, one reasonable decision at a time. It will be sustained or depleted by you, the same way.

The clocks were running before you arrived. They will keep running after you leave.

What matters is what you did while you were here.
