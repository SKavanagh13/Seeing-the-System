# Chapter 2: The Trajectory Clock

The protagonist has left their first budget briefing feeling more equipped than when they arrived. They know what "balanced" really means. They're asking better questions. But the closing note of Chapter 1 is still resonating: the annual budget is a one-year picture. A government could be managing that picture and still be drifting toward trouble.

The trajectory clock is running underneath the annual numbers. This chapter is about learning to read it.

Three questions will guide you through the trajectory clock. The first two look backward — at where you are and how you got there. The third looks forward — and shows the steward the greatest responsibility of all.

---

## Key Question 1: How do our assets compare to our liabilities?

The annual [GLOSSARY:budget]budget[/GLOSSARY] tells you what came in and what went out this year. But underneath that yearly flow, something else is accumulating — or eroding. The steward's question is: what is our actual financial position, and is it getting stronger or weaker over time?

### Why it's harder than it looks

Your government produces [GLOSSARY:annual_financial_statements]annual financial statements[/GLOSSARY], which can tell you a lot about the change in your position over time. In fact, it contains something called a [GLOSSARY:statement_of_net_position]statement of net position[/GLOSSARY]. This is [GLOSSARY:assets]assets[/GLOSSARY] minus [GLOSSARY:liabilities]liabilities[/GLOSSARY] and it is one of the most important signals available to you. When [GLOSSARY:net_position]net position[/GLOSSARY] is growing, that is genuinely good news. When it is shrinking, that is a signal worth taking seriously.

[L1]

### What every steward needs to know...

- Is our overall [GLOSSARY:net_position]net position[/GLOSSARY] — assets minus liabilities — getting stronger or weaker over time?

[/L1]

Like all signals, [GLOSSARY:net_position]net position[/GLOSSARY] as described in the [GLOSSARY:annual_financial_statements]annual financial statements[/GLOSSARY] has limits. No single perspective on public finance tells you everything — and the official accounting picture is built on rules that make sense for most governments most of the time, but they aren't perfect. A good steward knows how to look beyond the numbers.

### Behind the asset numbers...

- Land and buildings are carried at acquisition cost, not market value — meaning governments are often sitting on significant hidden value their financial statements don't reflect. Consider a public works facility built decades ago on what was then the edge of town. Today that site is surrounded by restaurants, hotels, and thriving development. The books still show it at its original acquisition cost. The real value — and the real opportunity — is invisible in the financial statements.
- More [GLOSSARY:assets]assets[/GLOSSARY] doesn't always mean a stronger position. Many public assets can't generate revenue and can't be sold — a local road will never have a toll and has no potential buyers. Yet it still requires ongoing maintenance.

### Behind the liability numbers...

- The most dangerous [GLOSSARY:liabilities]liabilities[/GLOSSARY] are the hidden ones: [GLOSSARY:postemployment_benefit_obligations]pension obligations[/GLOSSARY] that have been quietly underfunded year after year, and [GLOSSARY:deferred_maintenance]deferred maintenance[/GLOSSARY] on infrastructure that has no obvious price tag until something fails
- [GLOSSARY:deferred_maintenance]Deferred maintenance[/GLOSSARY] is the silent crisis — gradual deterioration makes the community less functional and less attractive, and catastrophic failure can arrive without warning

### Key questions to ask

- What is the condition of our major [GLOSSARY:capital_assets]assets[/GLOSSARY], and do we have an asset inventory that tracks how that condition is changing? What is our plan to fund maintenance of existing assets?
- For any new capital asset we are acquiring: what will it cost to operate, and where will that money come from?
- How did our [GLOSSARY:postemployment_benefit_obligations]pension liability[/GLOSSARY] change this year? Are we making progress or falling further behind? Are our assumptions realistic and up-to-date?

## Key Question 2: Are we following our own reserve policies?

[GLOSSARY:reserves]Reserves[/GLOSSARY] are one of the clearest windows into a government's financial discipline over time. The steward's question is simple but revealing: do we have a reserve policy, and are we actually following it?

### Why it's harder than it looks

[GLOSSARY:reserves]Reserves[/GLOSSARY] serve two distinct purposes that are easy to conflate:

- Insurance: a cushion against unplanned, unavoidable costs — a natural disaster, a recession, a sudden revenue shortfall. Without adequate reserves a government is forced into crisis decisions: slashing services, laying off staff, taking on emergency debt
- Savings: accumulated funds for projects or investments that wouldn't be affordable in a single year

The critical discipline is keeping these two purposes separate — and never raiding either one for [GLOSSARY:ongoing_expenses]ongoing expenses[/GLOSSARY]. Using reserves to balance the budget is the financial equivalent of eating your seed corn. It solves a short-term problem by making the next one worse.

Reserves are also an early warning signal for trajectory problems. A government quietly drifting in the wrong direction will often start drawing down reserves before anything else becomes visible in the annual numbers. Watching reserve levels over time is one of the most reliable ways to see the trajectory clock. Let's imagine reserves were used for a good reason, like responding to a natural disaster or paying for a previously planned [GLOSSARY:capital_assets]capital asset[/GLOSSARY]. The discipline is having and adhering to a policy for rebuilding of the depleted reserves.

[L1]

### What every steward needs to know...

- Do we have a formal reserve policy, and does it define a goldilocks zone for reserves — not too little, not too much?
- How do our actual reserve levels compare to our policy range, and what accounts for any gap?
- Are our [GLOSSARY:reserves]reserves[/GLOSSARY] being used for ongoing [GLOSSARY:operating_costs]operating costs[/GLOSSARY] — and if so, what is the plan to stop?

[/L1]

[BILLBOARD]How much is enough? It depends — and the answer is specific to your community.[/BILLBOARD]

- Have we analyzed our risks to set the "self-insurance" portion of the reserve at the right level?

- How have our reserve levels trended over the past several years?

## Key Question 3: Where are we actually headed?

The first two questions tell you where you are. This one asks where you are going. A government can have a reasonable asset position, adequate reserves, and a legally balanced budget — and still be on an unsustainable trajectory. The steward's capstone question is: do we know where we are headed, and is that direction sustainable?

### Why it's harder than it looks

- Many governments do not have a [GLOSSARY:long_term_financial_forecast]long-term financial forecast[/GLOSSARY]. Those that do often project past trends forward without accounting for changes in the environment that could alter those trends
- The annual budget focuses on the short term. The forces that determine long-term trajectory — demographic shifts, economic changes, aging infrastructure, [GLOSSARY:compounding]compounding obligations[/GLOSSARY] — operate on a much longer timescale and are easy to ignore when the annual numbers look fine
- A forecast is not a perfect prediction. It is a tool for thinking about the future — for surfacing assumptions, identifying risks, and making the consequences of current decisions visible before they become irreversible
- This is where the danger of [GLOSSARY:compounding]compounding[/GLOSSARY] becomes most visible. An unfunded [GLOSSARY:postemployment_benefit_obligations]pension obligation[/GLOSSARY] doesn't just sit still — it grows, the same way a credit card balance grows when you only make minimum payments. Small, reasonable annual decisions compound into large, structural problems that can take decades to unwind

[L1]

### What every steward needs to know...

- Do we have a [GLOSSARY:long_term_financial_forecast]long-term financial forecast[/GLOSSARY], and how far out does it look?
- Is the forecast projecting past trends forward, or does it account for changes in our environment — demographic shifts, economic changes, aging infrastructure?
- What does our forecast say about the sustainability of our current trajectory?

[/L1]

[BILLBOARD]A forecast tells you where you're headed. It doesn't tell you what could knock you off course.[/BILLBOARD]

- What are the biggest risks to our long-term financial health, and are they reflected in our forecast?
- What decisions are we making today that will be hardest to reverse later?

## Chapter closing — the reveal

You leave this chapter able to read the trajectory clock. You know how to assess your accumulated position, check whether your government is honoring its own commitments, and look forward to where current decisions are leading.

But there is one more dimension that neither the annual clock nor the trajectory clock fully captures. Both of those clocks are about your government's financial health during your time — your term, your career in public life, the years you can see from where you stand.

The generational clock asks a different question. It isn't about where you are or where you're headed. It's about what you are choosing to hand forward — to residents, taxpayers, and elected officials who aren't at the table yet.

That's the toughest clock of all. And it's where we're headed next.
